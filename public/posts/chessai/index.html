The Turning Point for Artificial Intelligence: Deep Blue vs. Kasparov

Exploring the historical match between Deep Blue and Garry Kasparov and its significance for artificial intelligence.
December 24, 2024

The Turning Point for Artificial Intelligence¶

Perhaps the first turning point for artificial intelligence occurred in 1997 when Deep Blue, a computer program developed by the American multinational IBM, defeated the reigning chess champion, Garry Kasparov. This is a controversial statement, but personally, I consider it an important milestone for two reasons.

Firstly, chess had been regarded for centuries as the ultimate expression of human intelligence. Therefore, the moment when an AI beat us at a task that many considered a true measure of “intelligence” marked the beginning of a continuous shifting of the goalposts when trying to define what machines must achieve to be considered intelligent. In other words, it was a humbling moment in our quest to understand the human mind and the very meaning of the word “intelligence.”

Secondly, this was the first time in history where advancements in artificial intelligence resonated with the general public. For several decades, the greatest science fiction writers of the century, like Isaac Asimov and Arthur C. Clarke, had captivated the masses with their writings and the endless possibilities that autonomous systems could bring. Now, the first tangible realization of their legacy had materialized in the eyes of the people: Kubrick’s HAL (ironically named after IBM) was here and had defeated the chess world champion.

Kasparov’s Kingdom: The Chess World Before Deep Blue¶

To properly illustrate this topic, it’s important to frame the chess world just before the match and understand who Garry Kasparov was, and still is, in the minds of those who follow the sport. Although chess has been played for many centuries, the Classical World Chess Championship was established in 1886. By 1997, only 13 people had held the World Champion title, including Kasparov (the number now stands at 16, and will soon be 17 since Magnus Carlsen is not defending his title in the ongoing Championship in Astana, Kazakhstan). Among these 13 individuals, Garry was widely considered to be one of the two players who could contend for the title of Greatest of All Time, the other being Bobby Fischer.

It is not the point of this article to delve into why they are considered above the rest; such debates are often controversial and subjective, depending on how one weighs different achievements. All the above means to point out how Kasparov was not an ordinary chess champion, rather, he was probably the best chess player that humanity had seen since the creation of the game, very superior to his peers at the time.

Some other interesting facts about Kasparov at the time provide context to understand his psychology going into the match. Prior to facing Deep Blue in 1997, Kasparov had never lost a chess match. This doesn’t mean he hadn’t lost individual games—like all chess champions, he had experienced many losses—but he had never lost a match, defined as a series of chess games played in succession between the same two players.

This was also not his first match versus Deep Blue; the previous year, they had played their first 6-game match, which Kasparov had won 4-2 with relative ease. He had also recently led a historic break-up in the chess world to separate the world champion from FIDE (the official chess governing body), creating a parallel institution to try to shepherd chess away from FIDE’s very apparent corruption. All this aims to point out that Garry was not the underdog in this fight. Kasparov was at the peak of his chess career, and he had agreed to this second match to assert his absolute dominance and, at the very least, prove that machines would not surpass humans before the turn of the millennium.

Chess and Compute¶

AI as a field was established in the 1950s by historic figures like John McCarthy and Marvin Minsky. Initially, chess appealed to AI researchers due to its complexity, clearly defined rules, and competitive nature. When we look at chess as a pure brute force problem, the average branching factor in chess, which describes the average number of legal moves that exist from a given position, is 35. This means that, on average, a computer only has to calculate ( 35^n ) positions, with ( n ) being the number of moves in the future it wants to calculate. The computing power of machines was increasing year by year, and although there were some other problems to solve, such as assigning values to a specific future position to determine whether it was desirable or not, it had been the limiting factor.

Some quite brave predictions were made during this time regarding AI progress in the coming years. Herbert A. Simon and Allen Newell, both of whom went on to win prizes such as the Turing Award (both) or the Nobel Prize in Economics (Simon), predicted in a 1958 publication that within 10 years…

“A computer will be the world chess champion.”

It is worth mentioning that today, in April 2023, that GOAT contender list would also include Magnus Carlsen, who will soon lose the World Champion title but remains nevertheless the undisputedly best human chess player alive today.
